Saddam Hussein was the president of Iraq from 1979 to 2003. How people judge him depends a lot on perspective—but here’s the clear, balanced picture:

What he was like as a leader

  • Authoritarian ruler: He governed with strict control. Political opposition was crushed; dissent often meant prison or death.
  • Strongman image: Promoted Iraqi nationalism and kept tight security over the state.
  • Human rights abuses: Responsible for mass killings, torture, and repression—especially against Kurds and Shia Muslims (e.g., the Anfal campaign).

Wars & conflicts

  • Iran–Iraq War (1980–1988): Extremely deadly; devastated both countries.
  • Invasion of Kuwait (1990): Led to the Gulf War and years of international sanctions that hurt Iraq’s economy and civilians.

Economy & society

  • Early years saw infrastructure and education improvements, funded by oil.
  • Later years were marked by sanctions, poverty, and isolation.

End of his rule

  • Overthrown in 2003 after the U.S.-led invasion.
  • Captured, tried by an Iraqi court, and executed in 2006.

Bottom line

  • To some Iraqis, he symbolized order and national pride.
  • To many others—and to most historians—he is remembered as a brutal dictator whose rule caused immense suffering.
